# Student Performance Analysis - Findings Report

## V1 — Gender Boxplots (Math vs Reading)

Looking at the boxplots, male students clearly do better in math compared to female students. The median math score for males is higher, which shows this difference quite well. When it comes to reading though, the situation flips female students have a noticeably higher median reading score than males. The spread of scores is pretty similar for both genders in math, so the gap is mainly in the middle. In reading, females show a bit more variation in their scores. There are a few outliers at the bottom in both subjects, which are students who scored much lower than everyone else. Overall, it seems like males lean more toward math while females are stronger in reading.

## V2 — Test Prep Impact on Math Score

Students who completed the test preparation course clearly scored higher in math compared to those who did not. The median math score for the completed group is noticeably higher, which shows that test prep does make a difference. The completed group also has less variation in scores, meaning most students who did prep performed consistently well. On the other hand, students with no test prep show more spread in their scores and a lower median. Both groups have some outliers at the lower end, but there are more in the none group. Overall, it looks like doing test prep is worth it when it comes to math performance.

## V3 — Lunch Type and Average Performance

Students who have a standard lunch perform better in all three subjects compared to students on free or reduced lunch. The difference is visible in math, reading, and writing — standard lunch students score around 10 points higher on average. This pattern is consistent across all subjects, which means lunch type seems to have a broad effect on overall performance. It could be that students with standard lunch have better nutrition or come from a more stable background. Either way, the chart makes it pretty clear that lunch type is linked to how well students do in school.

## V4 — Subject Correlations

Looking at the heatmap, all three subjects are pretty strongly correlated with each other. Reading and writing have the highest correlation of 0.95, which makes sense since both involve language skills. Math and reading are also strongly related at 0.82, and math and writing at 0.80. This means if a student is doing well in one subject, they are likely doing well in the others too. Overall, the three subjects move together quite closely, suggesting that general academic ability plays a big role across all subjects.

## V5 — Math vs Reading with Trend Lines by Test Prep

Looking at the scatter plot, there is a clear positive trend — students who score higher in reading also tend to score higher in math. This is true for both groups. Students who completed test prep are mostly scoring higher in math compared to those who did not, even at the same reading level. The two trend lines have a similar slope, meaning the relationship between reading and math does not change much with test prep. But the completed group line is slightly higher, showing that test prep does give a small advantage in math. Overall, reading score is a good indicator of how a student will do in math.
